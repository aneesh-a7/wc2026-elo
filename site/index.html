Why trust it — calibration

Accuracy is a trap: "always pick the favorite" already scores 56% on World Cup games, and the model scores the same. What matters is whether upsets happen as often as predicted. This reliability curve, from a leak-free backtest of the last five World Cups, is the real test.

Read it like this: each point is a group of matches where the model gave the favorite a certain win chance predicted. The vertical position is how often the favorite actually won observed. Points on the dashed line = perfectly calibrated. When the model says a favorite is only 35% likely, the underdog really does win about that often — the model takes the upset the right amount, instead of blindly backing the stronger name. Come back to that when you look at the 2026 scorecard above: three wrong picks out of sixteen isn't a broken model — it's close to exactly what a well-calibrated one should expect over one tournament.
